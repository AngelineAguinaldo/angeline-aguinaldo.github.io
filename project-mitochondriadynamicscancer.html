Mitochondria Dynamics in Aggressive Cancer Cells

Mitochondria are vital organelles needed to perform a myriad of cellular functions. These ‘powerhouses of the cell’ are found in both healthy and tumor cells alike. However, the role of mitochondria in the metastasis of a tumor cell is not well understood. New information has been found by Cecilia Caino and Dr. Dario Altieri at the Wistar Institute and his team [1], [2]. They have discovered a unique dependency of high invasive tumor cells on adaptive mitochondria dynamics.

Caino et al. were interested in conducting follow-up studies to understand how spatiotemporal variations in the mitochondrial network impact the invasiveness of prostate cancer cells. These studies involve the development of computational image analysis to quantify mitochondrial dynamics. It turns out that mitochondria shape changes, subcellular localization, and organelle trafficking vary in highly invasive versus low invasive tumor cells. The live prostate cells used for this analysis were labeled with red fluorescent protein (RFP) and imaged using a confocal microscope for 16 minutes with images acquired every 9.5 seconds.

An image analysis program was designed to quantify the number of fission and fusion events found in each movie. In each frame, the mitochondria were computationally segmented and tracked. Fission and fusion events were defined as the disappearance or genesis of a new track that fall within a minimum Euclidean distance from another mitochondria track in the post or previous frame. It was found that highly invasive cells exhibited more fission and fusion than low invasive cells.

Additional interrogation of the spatiotemporal distribution and median velocity of the mitochondria found that the highly invasive tumor cells contained mitochondria closer to the cell boundary and traveled at faster rates. This work is currently published in Nature Communications[3].

Project Details

This project was completed over the course of 16 months (September 2015 - December 2016). In this project, I was responsible for programming the image analysis software used (in MATLAB and MEX C/C++). The following software features were included:

  • Segmentation and tracking of mitochondria;
  • Quantification of fission and fusion events;
  • Calculation of mitochondria migration velocity;
  • Calculation of spatiotemporal distribution of the mitochondria; and
  • Automatic output of plots, movies, and annotated images.

Publication Release Date: December 19, 2016
