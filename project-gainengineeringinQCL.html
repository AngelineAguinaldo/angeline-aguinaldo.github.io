Gain Engineering using Interface Roughness Scattering in Quantum Cascade Lasers

What are Quantum Cascade Lasers?

Quantum Cascade (QC) lasers are semiconductor devices that are useful in remote sensing applications, such as non-invasive glucose monitoring and homeland security detective devices. They emit electromagnetic radiation through the recombination of electron-hole pairs within multiple quantum wells; a process called intersubband transitioning. The QC laser uses the intersubband transitions to generate its optical output. The intersubband scattering present during transitioning is responsible for the population inversion between upper and lower laser states. This relates directly to optical gain. Maximizing a laser’s optical gain will amplify the light that passes through the laser and minimize the power drawn by the laser.

Quantum cascade laser schematic illustrating how electrons and photons travel through the quantum wells

It is theoretically proven that some scattering can be attributed to the interface roughness (IFR) present at the quantum well interfaces[1]. Therefore, by strategically inserting a monolayer barrier with IFR within the band structure, the magnitude of intersubband transitions, and therefore gain, can be manipulated.

How did we prove it?

To support this claim, we designed three different structures, with specific monolayer barrier placements: (a) no additional barrier, (b) one barrier shortening the upper laser state lifetime, and (c) one barrier shortening the lower laser state lifetime, as shown in the figure below. Based on previous work of QC laser modeling, it is expected that the low inversion sample (b) display a ~40% decrease in gain, while the fast depopulation sample (c) display a ~30% increase in gain[1].

Positioning of the additional monolayer barriers in individual QCL designs. Their anticipated behaviors when compared to the initial baseline (a) would display lower population inversion (b), and faster depopulation (c)

Here we confirmed these theoretical predictions by observing the effects of the additional barriers on the differential resistance and optical emission using current versus voltage and electroluminescence (EL) spectrum measurements. It is known that gain is inversely proportional to the width of the EL spectrum [2]. A decrease in the half width-half maximum (HWHM) from the baseline to the fast depopulation design suggests an increase in optical gain as shown in the right most plot of the figure below and an increase in the HWHM from the baseline to the low inversion design suggests a decrease in optical gain as shown in the middle plot of the figure below.

Plot of electroluminescence spectrum for the baseline design (a), the low inversion design (c), and the fast depopulation design (b) at 80K and 300K. The HWHM is deduced from these plots

Based on these results, it can be seen that gain has been successfully engineered by using additional interfaces within the band structure.
